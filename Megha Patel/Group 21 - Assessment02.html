    Overview

    The product team PlanIT has been developing is a simple PC Power Supply (P/S) Tester that would allow even the most electrically illiterate people the capability to test their own power supplies at home safely. This reduces the risk to the average user who may have no formal training in using test equipment, such as a multimeter, safely and in a manner to reduce risk.

    The P/S Tester would be contained in a nonconductive case to with all circuitry contained inside and will be built to Australian and International Standards. The user can test their PC P/S but connecting the main outputs as specified by the P/S manufacturer to supply voltages to the standard ATX 24 pin sockets, utilising the P/S under tests' connection cables. The user will receive four green lights if the P/S tested is performing as required to power a motherboard.

    Function Description

    This functional description pertains to the prototype, as the product changes and more is added to the final revision, the descriptions will be added to this section.

    This P/S Tester takes the four standard output voltages from a conventional PC P/S and runs each one through a test, where on a pass a green LED will light to confirm the power output from the power supply meets a tolerance as documented. All voltages mentioned below are DC voltages.

    The +3.3V line runs through three in series diodes before going through the LED, with its anode towards source, in parallel to a 1kΩ resistor to ground. This will ensure that the power from output of the P/S is at minimum +2.8V.

    The +5V line runs in series through, a diode then a 3.3V Zener diode. These two diodes have their cathodes joined with their anodes facing to the other connection points. The line then connects through the LED, with its anode towards source, in parallel to a 1kΩ resistor to ground. The minimum voltage output from the P/S to illuminate the LED is +4.8V

    The +12V line runs through two 5.1V Zener diodes in series with their cathodes towards the source. The line then connects through the LED, with its anode towards source, in parallel to a 1kΩ resistor to ground. The minimum voltage output from the P/S needs to meet a minimum of +10.9V to illuminate the LED.

    The -12V line runs in series into two 5.1V Zener diodes with the anode towards the source. The line then continues through the LED, with its cathode towards source, in parallel to a 1kΩ resistor to ground. The P/S requires an output to reach a minimum of -10.9V before LED will illuminate.

    Tools and Software

    Multisim is a program that allows for the testing and development of circuits in a simulated environment that helps with finding any problems early on in the concept stage before production has begun. There is a free online version that can be used though the purchased product is recommended for more complex circuits, or if requiring more accurate results. The purchased product allows for testing of components to failure points.

    Eagle is an electronic design automation software (EDA) that allows for the design of printed circuit boards (PCB) from nothing, or even using a schematic as a starting point. This program can also export the final board design with routing or etching maps to various libraries that can be utilised by manufacturers to produce PCB's as required by the end user.

    AutoCAD is a computer-aided design software that allows for drafting, designing, and documenting of 2D and 3D drawings. Various aspects of this software can be used for different tasks, such as using AutoCAD for designing the outer case for the final product, which can be exported as a file to be utilised on a 3D printer. Alternatively, included in the software bundle, is AutoCAD Electrical which would be used to document and track changes to product schematics. Both this product and Eagle are produced by Autodesk and as such integrate with each-other easily.

    Any temperature-controlled soldering iron, which is employed in the production of proof-of-concept and prototype products. All connections are to adhere to AS/NZS 3000:20084, J-STD-001G5, and IPC-A-610G5 standards, with component tolerances documented in the schematics where required.

    Variable Power Generators allow for controlled testing of prototype circuits to prevent damage to both the prototype and removes risk of destroying a PC P/S while still in the testing stage. Also required to test for the tolerances of the final product.

    Required to ensure components, voltages, and resistances meet required tolerances as documented. As such these products would require a calibration routine to be established to ensure consistency throughout lifetime of the P/S testers product life. All ground points to be tested to ground pin with maximum resistance of 0.2Ω.

    Insulation Tester – Required for testing the product, allowing the assurance of meeting Australian standards. Testing of any cable is to be conducted at minimum twice the voltage of the line as specified in the schematic. Minimum resistance of any insulation test is to be no less than 1MΩ.


    Problems Arising and Improvements to Implement

    First improvement to be implemented is changing out the connector to be an ATX 24 pin power supply socket. This socket is a standard motherboard connector and as such the power supplied to each pin is consistent regardless of power supply. If testing a modular P/S then the user is to ensure they are using cables as supplied with the power supply. This change is required as there is no standard pinout for cables connecting to the power supply. As well as limiting the tester to only being able to test modular power supplies, the tester would be restricted to only testing power supplies that have matching connectors with identical pinouts. This is very restrictive as most manufacturers have their own various pinouts for their products.

    A lamp test to give the user the reassurance that if the power being tested is correct then the LED will light up. To implement this the tester would require an onboard battery to be added as well as its own circuitry to be designed and implemented, including the follow-on testing to ensure this doesn't reduce the reliability of the final product.

    An OK-To-Test lamp as an indicator to the user that they have established an adequate path to ground to allow for testing of the power supply to be conducted. Before the incorporation of this aspect, research is needed to ensure that there is always a path to ground through a connected power supply despite the power switch being turned off. If added to the final product then steps would be added to the operating procedures that will be made available to users.

    Current voltage test is based off all powers generated going to individual rails before outputting. If same voltage levels are made to different rails then product would need to be expanded to incorporate a test for each rail, possibly requiring additional plugs to test each rail. This is at least relatively easy to implement as the design for each voltage test would already be completed and would just need to be duplicated for an improved product with the expanded capability.

    Include a fused circuit near the input to protect the P/S Tester from stray voltages from a bad power supply. These fuses would need to be accessible and easily changed from the outside of the case, even if covered in the same manner as the batteries for most commercial products.
